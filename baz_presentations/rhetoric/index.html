Socratic Method

  • Seeks a contradiction in ones belief to discover a new stronger belief
  • Also used to find someone elses contradiction or hypocrasy in their own belief for the goal of devaluing their stance on a topic

Structure

  1. Socrates' interlocutor asserts a thesis, for example "Courage is endurance of the soul", which Socrates considers false and targets for refutation.
  2. Socrates secures his interlocutor's agreement to further premises, for example "Courage is a fine thing" and "Ignorant endurance is not a fine thing".
  3. Socrates then argues, and the interlocutor agrees, that these further premises imply the contrary of the original thesis; in this case, it leads to: "courage is not endurance of the soul". Then claims that he has shown that his interlocutor's thesis is false and that its negation is true.
  4. The new concept now considered is "Courage is a wise endurance of the soul"

  1. What goals do you want out of the Argument?
  2. Do you need to change the Audiences Mind, Mood, or Willingness to carry out a task?

Rhetoric

  • Is the Art of persuasion
  • No Rules(well, some)
    • Don't argue the inarguable
    • Don't turn the argument into a Fight

Modes for persuasion

Logos - Logic

  • Appealing to the Brain
  • Change their mind
  • Methodic, Locical, Stable

Ethos - Character

  • Appealing to the Gut
  • Persuade their "inituition"(I got a good feeling about this person)
  • Virtue, Practical Wisdom, Disinterest(aka Good Will)

Pathos - Emotion

  • Appealing to the Heart
  • Change their Mood, Get them to act
  • Empathy, Understanding, Connected

Offensive Rhetoric - Tense

Controlling the Tense is important. Using it wrong could turn an argument into a Fight or derail the argument

You can slot any kind of issue involving persuasion into one of the following categories:

  • Past
    • Blame
    • Forensic(who done it) - threatens punishment
  • Present
    • Values
    • Code Review, Code Guidelines
  • Future - Prefered
    • Choice
    • "Should we build or not build"
    • Promise of payoff
    • Deliberative Rhetoric

Tense

Good Arguer: We need to figure a way to deal with with scaling this solution up to work with large scale sets so for the next lego movie we won't have a performance issues

 Bad Arguer: If you came up with the same solution like you did last time it isn't going to scale

The Bad Arguer is talking in Past tense, blaming, wrong tense for this type of Argument. If the Bad developer doesn't switch to Future tense soon the argument is dead.

Bad Arguer: I agree that we will run into scaling issues if nothing is done. The choices I'm more comfortable with are A or B since it solves the problem I'm most worried about ....

Logical Fallacy

  • Bad Proofs include three sins:
  • False Comparison(Lumping examples into the wrong categories)
    • Kid: Why won't you drive me to school? All the other parents drive their kids to school
  • Bad example
    • Baz: This quote
  • ignorance as proof(asserting that the lack of examples proves something aka Argument from ignorance)
    • Giorgio A. Tsoukalos: "This sounds absolutly insane, but if you look into the whole story, along with the supporting e"
  • Wrong number of choices covers one essential sin, the false choice: offering just two choices when more are actually acailable, or merging two or three issues into one
  • Disconnect between proof and conclusion results in tautology(in which the proof and the conclusion are identical), the red herring (a sneaky distraction), or the wrong ending (in which the proof fails to lead to the conclusion)
    • Giorgio A. Tsoukalos:"Now this sounds absolutely insane, & I'm aware of how this sounds! But, if you look into the whole story, along with all the other supporting accounts, then, if you are of the same mindset, you could potentially come to the same conclusion"
